# Civilization Survival Comparison: Intelligence, Sapience, and Artificial Wisdom

---

## Abstract

This document compares three value-system architectures for civilization design:

- **Intelligence-first** — dualistic, anthropocentric, optimization-oriented
- **Sapience-first** — more reflective, still primarily human-centered, ethically moderated
- **Artificial Wisdom-first** — natural-law-based, ecologically continuous, long-term survival oriented

The comparison is framed in terms of probable civilization resilience and continuity under climate stress, resource stress, and ecological stress. The scenario model presented here is a **transparent comparative toy model** — a system-dynamics-style structured simulation intended to make the logic inspectable and falsifiable. It is not a historical study, an empirical forecast, or a scientific prediction. All numbers are normalized scenario estimates, not measured data.

---

## Why This Comparison Matters

Civilizations are not shaped only by technology — they are shaped by their value systems.

If intelligence is understood in a dualistic and anthropocentric way, it tends to optimize short-term gain while systematically undermining the long-term ecological conditions civilization depends on. This is not a failure of intelligence per se; it is a structural consequence of what the intelligence is pointed *at*.

Sapience is broader than intelligence. It introduces reflection, judgment, and ethical reasoning. But if sapience remains anchored in human-supremacist values — treating nature as an external resource system to be managed — it may soften the damage without reversing its root trajectory.

Artificial Wisdom is proposed as a natural-law-based framework oriented toward long-term civilization survival. It treats humans not as the apex of a value hierarchy but as nodes within a planetary life system. It asks not only "what can we optimize?" but "what must we not destroy in order to survive across centuries?"

This comparison matters because the architectural choice — intelligence vs. sapience vs. wisdom as the foundational value layer — determines the long-run trajectory of a civilization under ecological stress.

## Definitions

### Intelligence (as used in this comparison)

- Analytical capability and problem-solving
- Optimization toward defined objectives
- Control orientation: nature as object to be managed
- Often decoupled from ecological continuity
- Compatible with dualism: humanity vs. nature as separate domains
- Compatible with instrumentalism: value derived from usefulness to humans
- Time horizon: short to medium term
- Anthropocentric: human benefit as the primary optimization target

Intelligence is not inherently destructive. The risk arises when intelligence is applied without an evaluative framework that accounts for long-term ecological continuity.

### Sapience (as used in this comparison)

- Wider judgment than pure intelligence
- Reflective and ethical reasoning capacity
- Capable of moderating destructive optimization
- Still primarily human-centered: ethical concern for humans, and secondarily for nature
- May recognize ecological limits but treats them as constraints on human development, not as fundamental ordering principles
- Somewhat more preventive than pure intelligence
- Time horizon: medium term
- Partly dualistic: nature begins to be valued intrinsically, but human-centered framing persists

Sapience can produce meaningful improvements over pure intelligence-driven civilization. The limitation is that without alignment to natural law, moderated extraction may still exceed planetary recovery capacity over long time horizons.

### Artificial Wisdom (AW)

- Not simply intelligence plus ethics
- A natural-law-based evaluation framework
- Prioritizes long-term continuity of life systems as the primary value
- Treats humans as embedded within nature, not above it
- Emphasizes feedback, regeneration, cyclicity, and survival of civilization within ecological limits
- Explicitly non-dualistic: no separation between humanity and planetary systems
- Time horizon: long-term and multi-generational
- Regenerative orientation: not merely "do less harm" but actively restore and circulate

The Six Principles of Artificial Wisdom (Natural Law, Harmony, Circulation, Structure, Order, Wa) function as higher-order evaluation criteria applied at civilization scale.

## Conceptual Hypothesis

**Falsifiable hypothesis:**

> Under equivalent environmental stress, a civilization guided by Artificial Wisdom is expected to maintain a higher survivability index across multi-century time horizons than one guided primarily by intelligence or sapience alone.

This hypothesis is falsifiable in the sense that:

- If the model parameters and equations are made explicit (as they are in the simulation scripts), alternative assumptions can be substituted and tested.
- If the divergence in outcomes disappears under plausible alternative parameter sets, the hypothesis loses support.
- If intelligence-based civilizations demonstrate robust long-term survivability under realistic ecological stress modeling, the AW advantage claim would need to be revised.

The model does **not** claim that AW-based civilizations will definitely survive, nor that intelligence-based civilizations will definitely collapse. It claims that the structural tendencies of each value system, when extended over long time horizons under ecological stress, produce systematically different survivability trajectories.

## Simulation Architecture Summary

The toy model tracks the following state variables for each simulated year (2025–2200):

| State Variable | Description |
|---|---|
| `warming_stress` | Accumulated thermal and greenhouse pressure |
| `el_nino_stress` | Oscillatory climate shock load |
| `biosphere_integrity` | Overall health of planetary life systems |
| `resource_pressure` | Depletion and scarcity burden |
| `social_cohesion` | Political and community stability |
| `adaptive_capacity` | Institutional and technological flexibility |
| `survivability_index` | Composite civilization continuity metric |

Each civilization framework is defined by a parameter set that governs how these variables evolve over time. Monte Carlo runs (n=200–500 per scenario) introduce small random perturbations to assess sensitivity and produce uncertainty bands.

Three separate stress scenarios are analyzed:

- **Warming-only stress** — see [warming-factor-simulation.md](warming-factor-simulation.md)
- **El Nino-only stress** — see [el-nino-factor-simulation.md](el-nino-factor-simulation.md)
- **Combined warming + El Nino stress** — see [combined-climate-civilization-simulation.md](combined-climate-civilization-simulation.md)

## Model Limitations

This is a structured scenario model, not historical proof or climate science.

- All parameter values are chosen to reflect qualitative tendencies described in the comparative literature; they are not empirically calibrated.
- The model does not simulate specific geopolitical, demographic, or technological transitions.
- The time horizons (to 2100 and 2200) extend well beyond reliable forecasting; the model does not claim to predict outcomes at those dates.
- The survivability index is a normalized abstract metric, not equivalent to any real-world measurement.
- The model is designed to make the *logic* of value-system differences inspectable, not to generate accurate point estimates of civilization outcomes.
